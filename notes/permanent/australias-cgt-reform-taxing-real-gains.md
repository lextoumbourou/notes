Australia's capital gains tax rules are changing from 1 July 2027.

I'm just a lay person making sense of the new law, not even an accountant or anything.

## The old approach

Under the existing system, an individual, trust or partnership that held an eligible asset for more than 12 months generally received a 50% CGT discount. Half of the capital gain was added to taxable income and taxed at the person's marginal rate.

So if I bought a single stock of COMPANY for $100 and sold it for $200. That's would be $100 added to my taxable income. But if I held it for over a year, I would only add $50 to my taxable income.

The idea of the CGT discount, introduced by the Howard Government in 1999, was presumably to encourage long-term holding over short term speculation.

However, if you only made a profit on par with inflation (2-3% per year) you actually haven't really come out ahead in real money.

## What changes in 2027

From 1 July 2027, the 50% discount will be replaced with an inflation-based discount. The cost base will be indexed, so tax is directed at the gain above inflation: the real gain.

So, if you sold $100, but you held for 20 years, with inflation at 3.5% per year, the cost base of $100 would be adjust to $200 and you'd technically pay no tax.

However, the reform also introduces a 30% minimum tax on relevant capital gains (regardless of your tax rate). It applies only to gains arising after 1 July 2027, rather than retrospectively rewriting the tax treatment of gains that accrued before the start date. Investors in new builds will be able to choose between the former 50% discount and the new arrangement.

## My Opinion

I'm not really opposed to paying a reasonable tax rate, given a competant, funcionting democratically elected governtment. Literally the best investment you can make in your quality of life is paying for a services in the community you live in.

I find the indexed approach easier to reason about than a blanket 50% discount. If an asset rises only with inflation, its owner has not made a real gain in purchasing-power terms. Indexing the cost base recognises that.

At the same time, removing the automatic half-discount better reflects that a realised gain is income-like economic capacity. The reform does not say that every nominal increase should be taxed. It says that genuine gains should not automatically receive the same 50% concession regardless of their size, cause or the owner's income.

Some of my colleauges aren't happy about the impact to equity compensation - especially at startups, could become less attractive, and that can weaken incentives for risk-taking. I understand why founders, employees and investors are concerned about that. Tax settings influence behaviour, and Australia should care about making it possible to build ambitious companies here.
