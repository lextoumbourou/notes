In September 2022, kinda impulsively, I signed up for a Bachelor's Degree, advertised to me on Coursera. And now, after 3 years and around 9 months (with 3 of those months spent waiting for my results), I've finally completed my degree with First Class Honours. I'm edu-ma-cated, baby.

![My final assessment results](../_media/bachelors-after-hours/uni-assessment-results.png)

I wanted to write a short article about my experience. I'm sure someone out there in a similar position to me will find it useful.

## My Background

I've already had quite a long tech career without a degree, nearly 21 years, with around 14 of those years as a software developer.

So far, my lack of a degree hasn't been a barrier to my career. Partially, I think, because I live in a country that values experience and attitude over formal credentials. In fact, being "self-taught" is often seen as a positive, provided you appear to have actually taught yourself the skills needed to do the job.

That said, I'm not really "self-taught" per se. I've just learned outside of universities via online short courses (big shout-outs to David J. Malan's CS50 and Jeremy Howard's fastai), certificates, Kaggle competitions, and books. I've always had something to put in the Education section of my resume.

However, a lack of a degree has impacted my ability to work overseas. In my younger years, I made it to the final rounds of an interview with a US company I was interested in, only to learn that the E-3 visa, an Australia-US-specific agreement, requires at least a Bachelor's Degree. Though I have no intention of working overseas at the moment, it's nice to have the option if I need it.

Plus, I genuinely love learning, and I was interested in seeing where my knowledge gaps are. And also, it's an excuse to test out this [Zettelkasten Method](zettelkasten.md), which I like to go on about, with a real study problem.

Finally, I'm not getting any younger. I sometimes wonder if I should have got my degree in my 20s. Now, as I approach my 40s, I don't want to be saying the same thing about my 30s.

## University of London - World Class

The coursework for the degree is created by the University of London. The quality committee from Goldsmiths, University of London, reviews the material and continually refines it to ensure the degree meets worldwide standards. They also mark the assignments and exams.

Coursera hosts the lectures and provides resources such as lab notebooks and quizzes. They also provide forums to chat with teaching staff (which are rarely used), and this is how you upload your assignments.

The exams are done remotely using Inspera proctoring software. My understanding is that, at one stage before COVID, people actually went to local teaching centres for their exams. And there was also a time, just before ChatGPT got good at math, where they let you complete exams unproctored.

At the end, you get a degree from Goldsmiths - University of London.

I didn't shop around, I'll be honest. There might be better choices. But it suited my lifestyle nicely.

## Prerequisites and Performance-Based Admission

The prerequisites stipulate a [high school diploma](https://www.london.ac.uk/study/how-apply/am-qualified), which I don't even have. I didn't particularly like high school, and wanted to become independent as early as I feasibly could, which is a story for another day.

However, they offer an alternate route called Performance-Based Admission, which I think is a really clever idea. Basically, you sit two modules (Introduction to Programming I, plus one of the math modules), and if you pass them both, you're allowed to enter the full degree. The modules are counted towards your final grades, so it's not wasted time, but you get a good sense of whether the program is for you.

## Cost

Another thing that I loved is that you pay for the modules as you go. For me in Australia, a module currently costs £823 (about A$1,600), and the final project counts as a double module at £1,624. It was a bit cheaper when I started; fees creep up by about 5% a year. There are also small extras, like a £31 proctoring fee and an £11 admin fee per exam.

The University publishes the total programme cost as between £14,666 and £21,829, depending on your country of residence and pace. Mine landed at the lower end of that range, spread over almost four years.

Since this is education that's directly applicable to my career, it's also tax-deductible in my country. The ATO allows you to claim [self-education expenses](https://www.ato.gov.au/individuals-and-families/income-deductions-offsets-and-records/deductions-you-can-claim/education-training-and-seminars/self-education-expenses) when the study "maintains or improves the specific skills or knowledge you require for your current work activities". A Computer Science degree while working as a software engineer clears that bar, at least according to my accountant.

## Workload

The workload is pretty intense. Even if you're a veteran, like some of us are, with alumni working at Meta and tech leads of large corps, familiarity with the corpus helps, but you still have to do the work.

The assignments are often long and challenging. The exams are no cakewalk either.

They allow you to take up to 5 modules per session (or 3 plus the final project), and you have to complete them in 6 years, which requires about 2 modules per semester. Each module is rated at about 150 hours of study, so at my usual pace of 3 per session, that's roughly 20 hours a week on top of a full-time job.

It was quite a struggle, especially balancing challenging workloads as a CTO for one company, and now I'm at Canva, where there's a lot going on.

There were some really intense periods of my life where I would wake up at 4am, complete a three-hour exam, work through the day, then work on an assignment at night.

## Course Breakdown

The topics are pretty typical of a Bachelor's Degree in Computer Science, no surprises. Some math, although less than an engineering degree, and most things are quite hands-on.

My first two PBA modules were Introduction to Programming I and Discrete Mathematics. Introduction to Programming, while maybe a little beginner-level for me, was one of the best modules by far. They set up a series of programming challenges that you would submit, on top of a final project of a 2D platform game. Mine wasn't particularly special. My notes for [Discrete Mathematics Course Notes](../reference/moocs/coursera/uol-discrete-mathematics/index.md)

I specialised in Machine Learning and AI, which is also where my day job landed, so the back half of the degree lined up neatly with what I do for a living: Machine Learning and Neural Networks, Artificial Intelligence, Natural Language Processing, Intelligent Signal Processing, and Databases and Advanced Data Techniques, capped off with a final project.

The courses were a mixed bag: some genuinely excellent, others more of a slog. It's project-based, which suits me; you come out with actual work to show rather than just exam results.

## The Other Students

One of the best parts of the course is the company of the other students. Coursera invites you into a student Slack channel, which is basically a Lord of the Flies-style free-for-all, with no apparent official representation at all.

Some students took it upon themselves to run the Slack channel with an iron fist. Any post made in inappropriate channels would be met with immediate reprimand.

In fact, for many people, their first experience meeting fellow students was a rude message in `#general` for their introduction, when it clearly should have been posted in `#intros`.

There are really fascinating people from all over the world, and many in the industry, like myself. So you're actually networking with experienced people.

It's a remarkable community. The people who stick it out to the end tend to be extraordinarily resilient, from every walk of life you can imagine. I thought I had it tough, juggling the degree with full-time work (first at a startup, then at Canva). Then I'd read what some of my classmates were carrying.

One earned First Class Honours from war-torn Ukraine, working full-time and moving countries in the middle of it. Another had two babies during the degree. Another went through a divorce, a new baby, and a remarriage, and kept going the whole way. There were people from Meta, seasoned tech leads, and absolute beginners who'd never written a line of code.

And there's my friend Django, who's been completing his degree from a refugee camp in Uganda, powering his laptop off a solar panel and studying on mobile data. His story turned into a saga of its own: [Shipping a Laptop to a Refugee Camp in Uganda](shipping-a-laptop-to-a-refugee-camp-in-uganda.md).

Spend enough time in that Slack, and your own excuses get a lot smaller.

## AI Policy and The Evolution of LLMs

An interesting detail of my experience at UoL was that I started one month before ChatGPT was launched. Funnily enough, I wrote a little article about [Disputing a Parking Fine with ChatGPT](disputing-a-parking-fine-with-chatgpt.md) a few weeks into ChatGPT's launch, which I thought was kind of cute at the time, but now it seems so pathetically trivial it makes me laugh.

It had a significant impact on the course. Firstly, maths exams were much less proctored back then.

The questions were hard enough that they couldn't be Googled; there really wasn't a way to cheat. Even GPT-3.5 wasn't good enough to solve any difficult Computational Math questions. But as GPT-4 and Claude came along, that stopped being the case, and the university adapted, firstly by proctoring the exams using the Inspera software, and recently they've even done away with cheatsheets, presumably to prevent people from generating a cheatsheet with an LLM, or maybe smuggling in a second screen.

It also had a noticeable effect on the amount of conversation in the course channels. When I started, there was a lot of chatter, with people asking questions about the material and conversation ensuing, but that has had a noticeable decline. More people prefer to ask their questions to an LLM.

In Feb 2025, about 2.5 years into my degree, the uni launched an official AI policy. Basically, submitting LLM-generated work without acknowledgement is treated as contract cheating, the same category as paying someone to write your essay:

> "Submitting work which has been produced by software, or as the result of providing prompts or queries to any third-party service, either in full or in part and without acknowledgement, is a form of contract cheating. This includes the use of Large Language Model/AI chatbots." - General Regulation 7.9

Then, in October 2025, right as I started my final session, they went a step further and introduced a three-level framework for AI in assessment. Level Zero means no AI at all (think locked-down browser exams). Level One means you can use it for brainstorming and structuring, as long as you declare it. And Level Two means the assessment actually *requires* you to use AI, for example generating an output with it and then critiquing the result.

So over the span of one degree, the university went from having no AI policy at all, to treating AI submissions as cheating by default, to requiring AI use in some assessments.

My favourite detail: submitting false information generated by an AI, like a hallucinated reference, is an assessment offence in its own right (General Regulation 7.10). The university made hallucination punishable before most companies did.

## Other Questions

When I posted about this on Hacker News, nearly every question was practical, so here's the rundown.

**Is it legit?** Yes. It's a real BSc from the University of London, with exams and assignments set and graded through Goldsmiths. The certificate wording runs roughly: *"…having registered with the University of London and passed the approved examinations conducted by Goldsmiths College, has this day been admitted by the University of London to the Degree of Bachelor of Science."* On a resume it's fair to describe it as "Goldsmiths, University of London."

**What does it cost?** You pay per module, as you go, with no big sum upfront. All up, it's somewhere between £15,000 and £22,000 (roughly USD $20k to $25k), depending on where you live and how many modules you need. See the Cost section above.

**How long do you get?** You pick your pace each session, full-time or part-time, and you have to finish within six years. You can pause a session when life gets in the way, and you won't be charged for sessions you skip. I did it in about 3.5 years.

**Do you have to attend anything live?** No. The "classes" are just Coursera videos, and they're optional. If you're confident, you can go straight to the assessments. Each module has mandatory midterm assignments, then either a final exam or a final assignment.

**How do exams work?** Remotely, through proctoring software called Inspera. One gotcha: it runs on Windows and Mac, but not really on Linux (or in a VM without a fight). Worth knowing if you live in a terminal.

## Was It Worth It?

Yeah.

If you're trying to break into the industry, doing a degree like this part-time while you job-hunt is a genuinely flexible option, since you can dial the workload up or down each session depending on what's going on. For me, mid-career, it was never really about learning to code; it was about the credential and the option value.

But the thing I didn't expect to get out of it was the people. I finally have a Bachelor's degree after all these years, and I'd do it again for the Slack channel alone.
