# Gödel, Escher, Bach: An Eternal Golden Braid (GEB)

By **Douglas Hofstadter** (1979). Reading notes and reference.

## Preface to the Twentieth-anniversary Edition (1999)

The book opens with Hofstadter's own retrospective, apparently written to correct 20 years of misreadings. He complains that people treat GEB as a book about math/art/music/AI when it actually has a single subject, which is based around a thing he called the [Strange Loop](../../permanent/strange-loop.md).

The Strange Loop is a name for a mechanism that explains how animate beings arise from inanimate matter, or how a *self*, a soul, the word "I", emerges from a meaningless environment. The self is a strange loop.

[Gödel's Incompleteness Theorem](../../permanent/godels-incompleteness-theorem.md), which, at the time of reading and writing these notes, I'm not familiar with, is a formal system that demonstrates self-referentialism (or whatever), and that self-referentialism is the "loop" that Hofstadter uses to produce consciousness. Doug says that Escher and Bach weren't actually in his original drafts, but the connection came to him as he worked through it.

An interesting story about travelling to NY and back (perhaps a loop in itself?), whilst thinking about the story. And then the journal to writing the book, typesetting the book directly using early Stanford software (**TVEdit**), one of the first author-typeset books.

He encourages the reader to read it "correctly", playing along with the puzzles and the Achilles/Tortoise dialogues, not skimming to the "real" chapters.

And "strange loop" is in the translation of the book itself. Translating a book built out of English self-reference and wordplay was almost paradoxical; he delights in how translators solved it. Interestingly, Doug scoulds himself for using sexist "he" pronouns, but  redeems some of the pervasive generic "he" pronouns during the various translations.
